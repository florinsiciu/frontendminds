## 3. Content Production Engine

### The Reality Check

One high-quality decision-maker post requires 2,000-4,000 words of original, experience-backed content. That's 10-15 hours of writing for a solo operator who also runs a consulting business.

**Realistic cadence:** 2 substantial posts per month (not 1 per week).

### The Production Workflow

| Step | Who/What | Time |
|------|----------|------|
| 1. Outline + key points | Florin (experience + unique angles) | 1 hour |
| 2. First draft | AI-assisted (Claude/GPT with Florin's outline) | 1 hour |
| 3. Add real stories | Florin adds "I migrated 19 apps and here's what actually happened" | 3-4 hours |
| 4. Edit + optimize | AI-assisted SEO optimization + Florin final review | 1-2 hours |
| 5. Add schema + metadata | Technical SEO checklist | 30 minutes |
| 6. Create LinkedIn derivatives | 3-5 LinkedIn posts extracted from the blog post | 1 hour |

**Total per post:** 7-9 hours (vs. 15+ hours writing from scratch)

**The differentiator is Step 3.** AI can generate frameworks and structure. Only Florin can add the specific stories, numbers, and outcomes from 19 enterprise migrations. That's what makes the content rank — Google rewards experience signals (E-E-A-T), and AI answer engines cite sources with original data.

## 5. Signature Content Series

Individual blog posts don't build audience habits. Recurring series do.

### Option A: "Migration Monday" (LinkedIn)

- **Format:** Weekly LinkedIn post, every Monday
- **Content:** One specific lesson from Florin's migration experience
- **Length:** 150-300 words
- **Example:** "Migration Monday #1: The single question I ask every CTO before scoping a migration: 'What happens to your team velocity if we do nothing for 12 months?'"
- **Purpose:** Builds audience habit, generates engagement data to validate blog post topics

### Option B: "The Angular Modernization Report" (Monthly blog post)

- **Format:** Monthly state-of-the-ecosystem analysis
- **Content:** What changed in Angular this month + what it means for enterprise teams
- **Length:** 1,500-2,000 words
- **Example:** "May 2026: Signals adoption hits 60%, what this means for your RxJS migration timeline"
- **Purpose:** Recurring SEO content, positions Florin as the ecosystem analyst

### Option C: Serialized Case Study (Blog series)

- **Format:** Multi-part blog series
- **Content:** "Migrating App #19: Week by Week" — real (anonymized) migration story
- **Length:** 1,000-1,500 words per installment, 6-8 parts
- **Example:** "Part 1: The Audit — What we found in a 200K-line AngularJS codebase"
- **Purpose:** Demonstrates expertise through narrative, each installment is a separate indexable page

**Recommendation:** Start with Option A (lowest effort, fastest feedback loop). Add Option B or C once LinkedIn cadence is established and producing engagement.

## 6. Assessment as Content

Both the keyword research and analysis documents treat the assessment as a CTA endpoint. It should also be **content that ranks on its own.**

### What to Do

1. **Make assessment results shareable** — generate a unique URL for each completed assessment (e.g., `/assessment/results/abc123`) with OG image showing the score. People share scores on LinkedIn = free distribution.

2. **Publish the framework as reference content** — the 5-Dimension Framework page (Step 4 above) becomes the most linkable page on the site. Every blog post links to it. External writers cite it.

3. **Add "How scoring works" transparency** — a section explaining the methodology builds trust and gives AI answer engines structured content to cite when someone asks "How do I assess Angular codebase health?"

4. **Collect and publish aggregate data** — after 50+ assessments, publish "The State of Angular Modernization: What 50 Enterprise Assessments Revealed." This becomes the most citable asset on the site.

## 7. Format Discipline

### The Rule

The first 5 blog posts should all use the **same format**: experience-backed guide with specific numbers.

**Template:**
1. Hook: specific claim with a number ("In 19 enterprise migrations, I've seen this pattern fail 14 times")
2. Problem: what the reader is dealing with (written for their role, not for developers)
3. Framework: the structured way to think about it (named, citable)
4. Evidence: real examples from migration experience (anonymized)
5. Action: what to do next (including CTA to assessment)
6. FAQ: 3-5 questions from PAA mining, answered concisely (schema markup)

**Why one format:** Consistency builds production speed (Florin gets faster at this specific format), reader expectations (they know what they'll get), and SEO patterns (Google learns what kind of content this domain produces). Format experimentation is a luxury for sites with traffic.
